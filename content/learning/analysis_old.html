Series

A series is

Power Series

A power series is an infinite series of the form: $$\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n (x - c)^n $$ where $c$ is the complex center of the series, $x$ is a complex variable and $a_n$ is the $n$th complex coefficient.

The radius of convergence of a power series is the maximum range over which the series converges. Formally, we say that the radius of convergence $r$ is the largest nonnegative real number such that $|x-c| < r$ converges and $|x-c| > r$ diverges i.e. $$r = \sup \Big\{|x-c| : \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n (x - c)^n \text{ converges} \Big\} $$

One way to determine whether a series converges is to apply the ratio test, which considers $$L \defeq \lim{n \rightarrow \infty} \frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n} $$ and states that (a) if $L > 1$, the series diverges, (b) if $L < 1$, the series converges absolutely, and (c) if $L = 1$, the test is inconclusive.

Continuous Functions

Definitions of Continuity

Epsilon-Delta Definition of Continuity

Let $f(x)$ be a scalar function. We say that $f$ is continuous at the point $x_0$ if $$\forall \epsilon > 0, \exists \, \delta_{\epsilon} > 0 \text{ s.t. } |x - x_0| < \delta \Rightarrow |f(x) - f(x_0)| < \epsilon$$

Analytic Functions

A real function $f: x \rightarrow f(x)$ is analytic on the interval $I \defeq [a, b]$ if $\forall x \in I$, (a) the Taylor Series of $f$ converges, and (b) the Taylor Series of f is equal to f. Recall that the Taylor Series of a function f at a point $x_0$ is defined as $$T_f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{f^{(n)}(x)}{n!}(x - x_0) $$

Barbalat's Lemma

Consider a scalar, differentiable function $f(t)$. We might ask whether the following properties hold:

  • Does $\lim_{t \to \infty} \dot{f}(t) = 0 \Rightarrow f(t)$ converges? No. Consider $f(t) = \sin(\log(t)) \Rightarrow \frac{df(t)}{dt} = \frac{1}{t} \cos(\log(t))$. The time derivative converges to 0, but the function itself oscillates forever as a sinusoid.
  • Does $f(t)$ converges $\Rightarrow \lim_{t \to \infty} \dot{f}(t) = 0$? No. Consider $f(t) = e^{-t} \cos(e^{t}) \Rightarrow \frac{df(t)}{dt} = \cos(e^t)$. The function converges to 0, but the time derivative oscillates forever.

Given both these negative answers, Barbalat's Lemma aims to answer the question, under what conditions can we state that the derivative of a function vanishes? Before revealing the answer, we need to review a property called uniform continuity.

Barbalat's Lemma: Let $f(t) \geq 0$ be a scalar, lower-bounded function. If $f(t) \rightarrow$ limit $L$ as $t \rightarrow \infty$ and $\ddot{f} = \frac{d^2 f}{df^2}$ is lower bounded (or $\dot{f}$ is uniformly continuous), then $\lim_{t \to \infty} \dot{f}(t) = 0$.
