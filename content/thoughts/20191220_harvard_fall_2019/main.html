Harvard Fall 2019

Choosing Courses

Harvard offers students an unusual process for selecting classes. Rather than registering the quarter or semester prior, students spend the first week or two of the term fluidly attending any class that interests them. The university allegedly has plans to change this process since it's a nightmare for departments estimating professor load and the number of necessary teaching fellows, but I really like this. During undergrad, I had to rely on word of mouth and online reviews to evaluate a class before the quarter began, but here I was able to sample classes and decide for myself. Some lessons:

  • Harvard is hard. My program requires us to take 4 courses (or the equivalent) every term. I asked a student who was a year ahead of me what he recommended and surprisingly, he suggested taking 3 if I could get away with it. He said everyone comes to Harvard thinking highly of themself before discovering that the other students are very sharp and the courses will be very hard. He then told me every student who hears this lesson laughs it off, thinking "That might be true for others, but I'm going to prove myself tougher than them all!" The student then subsequently gets their ass kicked and wishes they had listened to the person with relevant experience. I decided to listen to his advice and he was correct (although, in fairness, I could definitely improve how I manage my time).
  • If you don't want to take a required class, fight like hell to get an exemption. I had a class that I was scared would become an uneducational time sink, and it took me until the 7th week of the term to receive permission to drop. But my friends who stayed in the class ended up hating it and complaining frequently about how much time the assignments took.
  • If you can't hear the professor from the middle of the room, don't take the class. As shallow as this sounds, maintaining attention over the term takes effort and a quiet, monotone professor is danger.
  • If the professor has never taught the course before, you might be in for a bumpy ride. Lectures may not wrapped into nice narratives, mistakes might proliferate through lectures and assignments. That said, I wouldn't recommend categorically avoiding new professors; I took AM226 Neural Computation (taught by Cengiz Pehlevan) and the course was amazing.
  • Ask the professor what his/her teaching philosophy is. I registered for a class that appeared to have all the right signals: enthusiastic instructor, amazing notes, exciting material. What I didn't realize is that her philosophy is to cater to the slowest student. The pace became excruciatingly slow and I stopped attending lecture altogether.
  • Tell the professor what your hopes/expectations are and ask whether they'll be met. One professor told me not to take her course because it was aimed for students with less technical backgrounds. I took her advice and appreciated it.

Courses

AM226 Neural Computation (Professor Cengiz Pehlevan)

10/10. I couldn't recommend this class highly enough to my friends, and at some point, they started getting sick of me mentioning it so frequently. Cengiz is an amazing speaker. It was his first time teaching the course, but everything flowed smoothly. His lectures were very clear and he would frequently pause to explain the intuition or significance behind a derivation we just walked through. The course requires familiarity with a number of fields including linear algebra, machine learning, statistical mechanics, differential equations and dynamical systems. I especially enjoy the problem sets (psets) since they typically tied together derivations with numerical simulations to demonstrate how theory and numerical methods nicely agree with one another. Many of the psets are taken from publish papers, but are written in such a way to help you through the problem e.g. Show that A leads to B. Now, assuming B is true, show that B leads to C, etc.

I did have a few problems with this class. First, the main teaching fellow was way too smart for me; I struggled to understand her explanations. Over the course of the term, I had to develop strategies for guiding her responses. For instances, I would ask about symmetry breaking in a model we covered in class, and she would reply, "Well, symmetry breaking is easier to understand in a different model that you've never heard of, that also won't be covered in this class." Second, at two points in the term, there were problems with timing such that office hours would either be hosted the day a pset was due or the day before a pset was due. I expect something like will be ironed out in future versions of the course.

AM205 Scientic Computing (Professor Chris Rycroft)

7/10. My opinion is divided on this course. I was required to take the course, but the material seemed irrelevant for theoretical work, so I started the quarter with quite a negative outlook. Chris is a fantastic person in person. He's enthusiastic and it's infectious. I would find myself getting really excited, and in general, that's what I want from my professors. The psets were diverse and it was quite clear that Chris invests a ton of time creating these interesting problems. One of my favorite aspects of Chris is that he color-coordinates every lecture: he shows up wearing a shirt that matches his terminal and his desktop, and he even brings a matching rubber ducky. At one point, he told us that he would unfortunately miss a lecture for a conference, but that one of his postdocs would cover for him, and that we shouldn't worry because she had promised to adhere to the color schedule. Two funny stories. Emily Xie and I wanted to pay tribute to his enthusiasm so we recruited our classmates to all wear purple shirts to the final lecture (which would be purpled themed) to surprise Chris. But the night before that lecture, Chris sent out an email, saying (paraphrased), "If you haven't noticed the color pattern by now, I'M GOING TO WEAR PURPLE TOMORROW AND IT WOULD BE REALLY COOL IF YOU ALL WORE PURPLE AS WELL." Emily and I also ordered 50 purple rubber ducks so everyone could surprise Chris, but much to our chagrin, we didn't check the delivery date and the ducks arrived the afternoon after the last lecture. We've since hidden the purple ducks throughout Maxwell-Dworkin and Pearce.

Unfortunately, this class had strong downsides. Chris is a slow speaker, so slow that staying focused becomes hard. The lecture material can be very dry and I'd guess that the vast majority of the material (>80%) is irrelevant on the psets, so paying attention becomes even harder. This is because there's a mismatch between the lecture material and the psets; the lectures are heavy on proofs of behavior, convergence, error bounds, runtime, etc., but the psets are heavy on implementation and plotting. The lectures are notationally heavy (unnecessarily so, in my opinion), in a way that invokes confusion. I recall him defining a variable, then proving a result that confused me; when I asked, he showed me that in the intervening slide, he had redefined the variable in a critical way that wasn't highlight whatsoever. One aspect of the psets that I despised is that problems cascade e.g. you can't start part b without finishing part a. I feel this adds unnecessary stress without any clear pedagogical purpose. Some of the teaching fellows were also famous for giving out solutions if you asked, which devalued the grades in my eyes. I don't think Chris knew this whatsoever, but oddly, only his TFs did this. To end on a positive note, Chris is significantly better in office hours than he is in lecture. He's also very responsive and patient in responding to students on Piazza.

AM207 Stochastic Methods for Analysis, Inference and Optimization (Dr. Weiwei Pan)

6/10. This was my least favorite class of the quarter. To add a caveat, Dr. Pan is a very enthusiastic lecturer and her slides are aesthetically attractive; together, many of my friends thought that this was the best course of the term. I disagreed. In my opinino, Dr. Pan lectures to the slowest student in the class, repeating the same simple points over and over, within and across lectures. Her presentations are poorly structured, content-wise, in that they fail to provide conceptually useful high level viewpoints that make understanding the material easy. Three examples. First, almost all the probability distributions covered were presented in their univariate forms, obscuring insights that the multivariate forms might offer. Second, the notion of the exponential family wasn't mentioned until something like the fourth week, even though it offers a concise, clean way to understand the distributions we had discussed up till then. Third, when covering variational autoencoders, she could have easily explained the concept by saying "autoencoder plus (variational) evidence lower bound loss function." Instead, she pretended that we hadn't had several lectures on variational inference before and started from scratch all over again. I felt like we could have covered significantly more material within the time limits. The assignments were decently cool, though.
